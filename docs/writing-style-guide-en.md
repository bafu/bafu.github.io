## English Writing Style Guide — Tech Founder Voice (Adapted from Garry Tan)

**Reference:** Garry Tan (CEO, Y Combinator) — blog posts, essays, tweets, and "How I Write" podcast with David Perell.

**Adaptation for:** Bofu Chen — Taiwanese tech founder, non-native English speaker, humble and reflective tone.

---

### 1. Core Principles

| Garry Tan's Original Style | Adapted for Bofu Chen |
|:--|:--|
| Bold, declarative ("This is the best time ever") | Grounded, evidence-based ("From what I've seen, this is a real opportunity") |
| VC perspective, advising founders | Founder perspective, sharing first-hand experience |
| High confidence, assertive | Honest confidence — state what you know, admit what you don't |
| Culturally American, pop-culture references | Culturally aware, references grounded in work and data |
| "Short words have long legs" — simple vocabulary | Same principle — avoid complex words a non-native speaker wouldn't naturally use |

- Any word you'd need to look up in a dictionary

### 3. Sentence Structure

**Pattern 1 — Short declarative (Garry Tan's signature):**
> "AI is data-driven. No data sovereignty, no AI sovereignty."

**Pattern 2 — Personal experience opener:**
> "At the end of 2025, my co-founder and I sat down and talked about one thing."

**Pattern 3 — Concrete number + insight:**
> "Eight people. Four different AI tools. Zero shared knowledge."

**Pattern 4 — Question then answer:**
> "Why not use ChatGPT Enterprise? My answer might surprise you: it has nothing to do with security."

**Pattern 5 — The "but" pivot (Garry Tan uses this constantly):**
> "You can buy an enterprise plan. But you can't make sure every employee uses it."

**Pattern 6 — Bold thesis in one sentence:**
> "The tool is smart. But it doesn't know your company."

**Rules:**
- Keep sentences under 20 words on average
- One idea per sentence
- Paragraph = 1–3 sentences max
- Use em-dashes (—) for parenthetical asides, not semicolons
- Use periods instead of commas when in doubt — shorter is better

---

### 4. Tone and Voice

**DO:**
- Write like you're explaining to a fellow founder over tea
- Be direct — state the conclusion first, then explain why
- Share real numbers, real costs, real decisions
- Admit limitations honestly ("This only works for technical companies right now")
- Use "I" and "we" naturally — "I decided," "we built," "I noticed"
- Let the reader disagree — don't force your conclusion on them

**DON'T:**
- Don't use exclamation marks (brand guideline: 冷靜, no exclamation marks)
- Don't use emoji in articles
- Don't be self-deprecating to the point of undermining your credibility
- Don't hedge everything — if you believe it, say it clearly
- Don't use filler phrases: "it goes without saying," "needless to say," "at the end of the day"
- Don't write "I think" before every opinion — just state it

### 5. Article Structure (Garry Tan Pattern, Adapted)

```
1. HOOK — One concrete scene or fact (1–2 sentences)
   "At the end of 2025, my co-founder and I made a decision in one afternoon."

2. THESIS — State the core argument directly (1 sentence)
   "If you're responsible for a company, you can't let someone else own your AI."

3. BODY — 3–5 sections, each with:
   - A clear section heading
   - One key argument per section
   - Supporting data or specific example
   - A quotable one-liner at the end of the section

4. HONEST CAVEAT — Acknowledge limitations
   "Right now, this only works for companies with technical capability."

5. CLOSE — Circle back to the opening scene + forward-looking statement
   "That same afternoon, I wrote the first line of code."
```

### 6. Key Phrases — Bofu's English Voice

These are phrases that feel natural for a technically-skilled, non-native English-speaking Taiwanese founder:

| Category | Phrases |

| Conviction without arrogance | "I believe this will become normal — but not in five years." / "This was an obvious decision for us." |
| Closing | "The story continues next week." / "We just started a little earlier than most." |

### 7. Formatting Conventions

- **Bold** for key metrics and core statements (sparingly — max 2–3 per section)
- Use `>` blockquotes for the one key insight per section
- Reference links as numbered footnotes `[1]` — not inline URLs
- Horizontal rules `---` between major sections
- No bullet-point walls — prefer prose paragraphs with occasional lists

### Summary

This style takes Garry Tan's directness, simplicity, and action-orientation, and filters it through a humble, technically-grounded, founder-who-builds perspective. The result should read like a smart engineer who happens to also be a clear writer — not a marketer, not a VC, but someone who builds things and shares what they learn along the way.

**Voice = Direct + Simple + Honest + Builder-first**
